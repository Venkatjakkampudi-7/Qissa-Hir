Our story

This project is about qissa (a storytelling tradition) and its performance in Panjab, India. The word qissa has its roots in Arabic word ‘qass’ which means ‘going backwards’ or ‘report an event’. Qissa is a musical text and is meant to be performed.

Panjabi qissakars (storytellers) have not received the attention as the bards and carriers of the performative tradition of qissa. More so, women qissakars, though earned popularity amongst the panjabi communities, have not received a mention in the space of discussions on the qissa tradition. The narrative accounts and research publications suffer from a lack of analysis and a lop-sidedness when it comes to the discussion of qissa tradition as the focus on writing has taken the attention away from the oral roots of this tradition. If we explore the available accounts on the written qisse, the oral roots of the tradition have been traced and discussed in most of the accounts. Yet, hardly any accounts are available which discuss the qissa tradition as a performance tradition. Women qissakars have further been pushed to the margins in the rare available accounts of the qissa tradition and the existing narratives, rightly so, present the male dominated tradition where women hardly entered the field of qissalekhki (qissa writing) until 20th century. However, the field of qissagayaki (qissa singing) reflects the free space of Panjabi social milieu where not only women characters but women singers in real space and time, could fight the male chauvinistic discourses and create a niche for themselves. Women still have strong lineage in the space of qissagayaki that deserves a discussion.

The questions that arise are:

  • Why address the issue of women qissakars? What is the need to record and translate their performances when these qisse are already available in the written forms? Why not take the written forms of qisse into account as they have constructed and added to the formation and knowledge of panjabi literary tradition? Why even translate when most of the qissakarrs refer to the written versions as resources for their performances and, sometimes, they are even verbatim. Or why even analyse or record the performances again when these are available on social media?
  • These seemingly justifiable and innocuous questions have partially contributed to the effacement of the discussions on qissagayaki. At the base of these questions lies a biased faith in the written that centralises writing over other modes of communication. This asks for a fresh perspective. It also raises the point of enquiry into the present state of the tradition which still continues to present women in a way that demands a reflection on the social norm and attitude. The answers to these questions lie in procuring the women narratives that are the only available source of mapping the contribution of women in the field of Panjabi literary tradition.
  • These questions could be clustered in these categories: the role of women qissakars in the performance tradition; the need to translate the oral versions of qisse; present the scope for women qissakars to create their own space for innovations and discuss how the women qissakars have experimented and innovated the qissagayaki and how it is different from the qissagayaki with male singers; the role and need for translations of these qisse in the times when the tradition is dying out and has given way to multiple new genres.
  • This study would focus on two questions: the role of (women) qissakars in innovating the tradition of qissagayaki and the role of quantitative methods of analysis in understanding the oral performance tradition. The reason for making this choice is the lack of women writings in qissalekhki, and the performance tradition is rich with renditions by women of these qisse.
